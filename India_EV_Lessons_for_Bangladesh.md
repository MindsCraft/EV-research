# 🇮🇳 India EV Charging Market — Lessons for Bangladesh
### Comparative Analysis | June 2026

> India is Bangladesh's most relevant benchmark market. Same heat, similar grid instability, price-sensitive customers, rapid Chinese EV adoption, and government-driven targets. They are approximately **4–5 years ahead** of Bangladesh. Every mistake they made is a lesson we can skip.

## 🏢 India's Major Players — Who They Are & What Happened

### 1. Tata Power EZ Charge — The Market Leader Who Hit a Wall

**What they did right:**
- First mover, brand trust from Tata Group
- Deployed at premium locations (malls, offices, highways)
- Strong app with payment integration

**What went wrong:**
- Expanded too fast without demand validation
- Some stations running at **<5% utilisation**
- Paused expansion in 2025 to focus on profitability
- Scale without maintenance = broken chargers everywhere

> **Bangladesh lesson**: Don't deploy 100 chargers before you have 100 regular users. **Utilisation beats quantity.** A network with 10 working chargers beats 50 broken ones.

---

### 2. Ather Grid (Ather Energy) — The Fleet-First Success Story

**What they did right:**
- Built charger network *alongside* their own scooter
- Prioritised their own vehicle ecosystem first
- High-quality hardware, strong brand consistency
- Users trusted it because it "just worked"

**What went wrong:**
- Blocking issues when other vehicles used their chargers
- Occasional session timeouts requiring manual reset

> **Bangladesh lesson**: **Tie-in with a vehicle brand first.** Approach BYD Bangladesh or Deepal dealers — build your charger network around *their* customers. Guaranteed initial utilisation.

---

### 3. Statiq & ChargeZone — The Partnership Model Winners

**What they did right:**
- Did NOT try to own land — partnered with hotels, malls, BPCL petrol stations
- Asset-light model: partner provides land, they provide hardware + software
- Focused on high-dwell locations (people stay 1–3 hours)

**What went wrong:**
- Maintaining chargers across many partner locations is operationally hard
- Quality inconsistent when partner staff don't understand the equipment

> **Bangladesh lesson**: **Hotel, mall, and corporate campus partnerships = your fastest path to utilisation.** You don't need to own land. Find a Radisson or a corporate park and offer revenue share.

---

### 4. Jio-bp Pulse — The Oil Major's Entry

**What they did right:**
- Leveraged existing Reliance petrol station land and power connections
- High-power DC fast chargers (60kW+) with strong brand awareness
- Nationwide coverage fast

**What went wrong:**
- Non-EV cars parking at charger spots (ICE blocking)
- Network density still insufficient on key corridors

> **Bangladesh lesson**: **Padma filling stations, Meghna fuel stations** — approach them. They already have the land, grid connection, and footfall. You just bring the charger + software.

---

### 5. Indian Oil / BPCL / HPCL — Government-Backed Rollout

**What they did right:**
- Massive scale, government mandated
- Existing infrastructure (power, land, staff)

**What went wrong:**
- Bureaucratic slowness
- Chargers installed that nobody used (wrong locations)
- Staff at petrol stations don't know how to troubleshoot chargers

> **Bangladesh lesson**: **Petrobangla / BPC** may eventually get into this space. Position yourself as a private sector operator that fills gaps faster than SOEs can.

## ❌ India's Top Failures — Don't Repeat These

### Failure 1: The Broken Charger Crisis
**What happened**: By 2025, ~72% of EV users had experienced a broken or unavailable charger. DC fast charger uptime sometimes as low as **40%**. Users stopped trusting public networks.

**Root causes:**
- No proactive maintenance system
- Connectors broke from rough handling, no replacements stocked
- Software "handshake" failures never remotely resolved
- No 24/7 monitoring

**Bangladesh application:**
> Build your maintenance system **before** you need it — not after your first charger breaks. You need: spare parts inventory, a local electrician on retainer, remote OCPP monitoring dashboard, and a WhatsApp support number on every charger.

---

### Failure 2: The Payment Fragmentation Disaster
**What happened**: Every CPO built their own app. Users needed 5–6 apps for different networks. Payment failures on **28% of attempts**. QR codes faded, RFID readers broke.

**Bangladesh application:**
> Launch with **QR + RFID + WhatsApp/bKash payment** from day one. Bangladesh users will not download a new app for one charger. Payment must work on the first try, every time.

---

### Failure 3: Wrong Location, Zero Utilisation
**What happened**: Stations were installed where the government said, not where EVs actually went. Remote locations, wrong neighbourhoods, highway spots with no shelter.

> Tata Power had stations at **<5% utilisation** — charging 1–2 cars per day on a unit that cost ₹5–10L to install.

**Bangladesh application:**
> Do not guess locations. **Follow the EVs.** BYD Atto 3 buyers live in Gulshan, Banani, Dhanmondi, Bashundhara. Put chargers where those people park. A hotel carpark in Gulshan will utilise a charger daily. A highway in Tangail will not — yet.

---

### Failure 4: Grid Shock
**What happened**: Multiple DC fast chargers at one location caused transformer overload, local outages, and angry building managers. DISCOMs in India were not prepared for 150kW+ point loads.

**Bangladesh application:**
> Always get a **load sanction letter** from DESCO/DPDC before installing any charger above 7kW. For DC fast chargers, you need a dedicated transformer assessment. Bangladesh grid is weaker than India's — this matters more here.

---

### Failure 5: "Install and Forget" Syndrome
**What happened**: Operators focused entirely on growing station count (because government subsidies were per station installed), not on keeping stations working. Result: ghost chargers — present on the map, broken in real life.

**Bangladesh application:**
> Your KPI should be **uptime %, not station count.** Track every charger remotely via OCPP. If a charger goes offline, you know in minutes, not weeks.

## ✅ India's Successes — Copy These Directly

### Success 1: Fleet-First Model Works
Indian operators who targeted **corporate fleets and ride-hailing** (Ola Electric, BluSmart) had the highest utilisation rates. A fleet of 50 EVs charging nightly = guaranteed revenue.

> **Bangladesh action**: Approach Pathao, Shohoz, or corporate shuttle fleets in Dhaka. Offer them a dedicated charging solution. Guaranteed 6–8 hours of charging per vehicle per night = your charger pays itself off fast.

---

### Success 2: Destination Charging Outperforms Highway Charging (Early Stage)
Hotels, malls, offices, and hospitals had **3–4x higher utilisation** than highway-only stations in India's early phase. People charge while they're doing something else.

> **Bangladesh action**: Target Westin, Radisson, Le Méridien, Dhaka — their guest profile is exactly the BYD/Deepal buyer. Corporate offices in Gulshan/Banani. These locations need charging for their parking and you need utilisation. Perfect match.

---

### Success 3: OCPP + Open Platform = Stickiness
Operators who adopted OCPP 1.6J/2.0.1 from the start could:
- Switch hardware suppliers without rebuilding software
- Offer roaming to other networks (OCPI protocol)
- Add features remotely via firmware updates
- Attract B2B clients who want data integration

> **Bangladesh action**: This is your direct expertise advantage. You understand OCPP from the backend. Build your platform on open standards from day one — don't lock yourself into one hardware vendor's proprietary cloud.

---

### Success 4: Tiered Pricing = Revenue Optimization
India's mature operators now use:
- **Off-peak**: Lower rate (incentivize night charging, less grid stress)
- **Peak**: Standard rate
- **Fast charger premium**: Higher rate (DC fast = premium experience)
- **Idle fee**: Charge per minute after charging completes (frees up bays)

> **Bangladesh action**: BERC regulates energy at BDT 11.36/kWh — but you can add a **service charge** on top. Design your pricing tiers from the start. An idle fee (BDT 2–5/min after full charge) will save you from ICE blocking and revenue loss.

## 🎯 The 5 Rules for Bangladesh (Derived from India's Experience)

### Rule 1: Uptime Over Scale
> "One reliable charger in the right location beats ten broken chargers anywhere."
- Target **95%+ uptime** from day one
- Monitor every charger remotely via OCPP
- Stock spare contactors, cables, and RFID readers

### Rule 2: Location Wins Everything
> Site selection is the most important business decision you make.
- **Tier 1 targets**: Gulshan, Banani, Dhanmondi, Bashundhara (BYD buyer neighbourhoods)
- **Tier 2 targets**: Corporate offices, hotels, hospitals, shopping malls
- **Later**: Highway corridors (Dhaka–CTG, Dhaka–Sylhet)

### Rule 3: Fleet Before Retail
> Fleet clients = guaranteed utilisation = predictable revenue
- Target Pathao, Shohoz, BluBus, corporate shuttles
- Even 5 vehicles charging nightly = BDT 15,000–25,000/month per charger

### Rule 4: Payment Must Be Frictionless
> App downloads are a conversion killer in Bangladesh
- Launch with: **bKash QR + RFID card + walk-in cash payment option**
- Never require app download to start a charge
- Bill by kWh not by time — fairer and more trusted

### Rule 5: Open Platform from Day One
> Your OCPP knowledge is your moat — use it
- Run your own OCPP backend (or white-label Ampeco/CitrineOS)
- Never tie to one hardware vendor's proprietary cloud
- Build API integrations: bKash payment, Google Maps listing, CRM

## 💡 The Biggest Opportunity India Revealed

**The gap is not hardware. It's software and service.**

India proved that the hardware (chargers) is a commodity. The real value — and the real moat — is:
1. **Reliable uptime** (maintenance + monitoring)
2. **Seamless payment** (bKash/Nagad in Bangladesh)
3. **Fleet management integration** (for B2B clients)
4. **Usage analytics** (which chargers are profitable, which aren't)
5. **Remote diagnostics** (fix issues without sending a technician)

All five of these are **software problems**. And you have a software background. This is the exact gap that Ekhon Charge and Crack Platoon — Bangladesh's current operators — are not filling.
