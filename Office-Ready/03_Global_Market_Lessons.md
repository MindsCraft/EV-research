## Why Study Other Markets?

Bangladesh's EV charging industry in 2026 is approximately where India was in 2020–2021, Vietnam in 2021, and Indonesia in 2022. Every one of those markets has already made the mistakes — and found the breakthroughs — that we are about to navigate. Reading these lessons correctly is worth more than any consultant's report.

**Three things every market proved, without exception:**
1. The operator who shows up early and secures the best locations wins permanently
2. Hardware without reliable software and billing is just a broken box on a wall
3. Uptime — not quantity — determines whether drivers trust and use your network

## 🇮🇳 India — The Reliability Crisis

### Market Status (2026)
India's EV charging market has grown to tens of thousands of public chargers, driven by government FAME-II subsidies and private investment from operators like Tata Power EV, BPCL, ChargeZone, and Statiq. However, by 2026, the industry has entered what analysts now call the **"Optimization and Reliability Phase"** — a period of painful reckoning after a deployment-at-all-costs era.

### What Went Wrong

**1. The Uptime Collapse**
The single most damaging failure in India's EV charging rollout was prioritising charger count over charger functionality. By 2026, a significant portion of publicly listed chargers are non-functional, offline, or show incorrect status on apps. In some cities, drivers report that fewer than 50% of chargers on map apps actually work when they arrive. This has created a deeply embedded distrust of public charging that is actively slowing EV adoption — even as the hardware sits installed.

**The Bangladesh lesson:** Operational reliability is not an after-launch concern. Every charger we deploy must have remote diagnostics, a maintenance SLA, and a spare parts pipeline before it goes live. One broken charger in a visible location damages our entire brand.

**2. The Utilisation Trap**
Most Indian public chargers operate at under 25% utilisation — meaning they sit idle for 18+ hours per day. Operators who deployed without verifying actual local EV density are now servicing debt on assets that generate negligible revenue.

**The Bangladesh lesson:** Site selection is a financial decision, not just a commercial one. Before committing to any location, we need to confirm: How many EVs are registered within a 5km radius? What is the likely dwell time? Is the host willing to promote the charger to their customers? A charger at the wrong location is a liability, not an asset.

**3. Grid Permitting — The Silent Project Killer**
In India, grid interconnection approvals from DISCOMs (utility companies) caused project delays of 3–9 months at many commercial sites. The process was opaque, inconsistent across states, and required relationships that most CPOs had not built.

**The Bangladesh lesson:** DESCO and DPDC approvals in Dhaka are the equivalent risk. Every commercial installation above 7kW requires a load sanction. These must be initiated *before* hardware is ordered, not after. Build DESCO/DPDC relationships as an early priority.

**4. The App-Download Barrier**
Indian CPOs who required app downloads before a driver could charge saw dramatically lower session rates than competitors who offered QR-code web-based payment. The conversion drop-off between "I want to charge" and "I have the app installed and payment set up" is enormous for first-time users.

**The Bangladesh lesson:** The driver experience must begin at the charger, not at an app store. A QR code that opens a mobile web page, shows the rate, and accepts bKash/Nagad/card in under 60 seconds — with no download required — is not optional. This is the baseline for public-facing chargers.

### What Worked in India

- **Oil company partnerships:** Operators who partnered with HPCL, BPCL, and IOC for their forecourt locations gained instant high-footfall access that pure-play operators could not match
- **B2B fleet contracts:** Corporate fleet charging (for logistics and cab aggregators) provided predictable, bankable revenue that de-risked early deployments
- **High-traffic mall anchors:** Shopping mall operators who actively marketed the charger amenity drove 3–5x higher utilisation than passive installations

## 🇻🇳 Vietnam — The Infrastructure Moat and the Monopoly Warning

### Market Status (2026)
Vietnam's EV market is dominated almost entirely by VinFast and its charging subsidiary **V-Green**, which has deployed over **150,000 charging ports across all 63 provinces**. By mid-2026, V-Green is executing a second wave: 99 ultra-fast charging hubs (150kW+) along 34 key national highway provinces, ensuring no EV driver on a major route is ever more than 50km from high-power charging.

### What VinFast Did Right

**1. Infrastructure before mass adoption**
VinFast began building charging infrastructure before they had significant vehicle sales. They eliminated range anxiety as a purchase barrier *before* customers needed to confront it. The result: VinFast maintains dominant market share and their customers feel no incentive to switch brands partly because the charging network only works with their cars.

**The Bangladesh lesson:** In Bangladesh, Runner Automobiles' BYD partnership is bringing a serious EV brand to market. The charging network that serves BYD drivers first — and does it reliably — will earn loyalty before competitors can respond. Speed of deployment matters.

**2. Geographic coverage creates psychological safety**
Vietnam's 63-province coverage means an EV driver can travel from Hanoi to Ho Chi Minh City (1,700km) without range anxiety. This national coverage changed the cultural conversation about EVs from "risky experiment" to "mainstream transportation."

**The Bangladesh lesson:** We do not need national coverage on Day 1. But we need enough density in Dhaka — with visible, working chargers at recognisable locations — to create the same psychological shift in the capital first. Critical mass in Dhaka before expanding.

### What Vietnam Got Wrong

**1. The closed ecosystem trap**
VinFast's chargers are exclusively for VinFast vehicles. This means every BYD, MG, Wuling, or imported EV driver in Vietnam has no access to the dominant charging network. Independent CPOs struggle to justify investment because the largest pool of potential users is locked out.

**The Bangladesh lesson:** Our OCPP-open network is a strategic advantage, not just a technical choice. By supporting all EVs on a neutral platform, we capture demand from every brand — BYD, Hyundai, KIA, MG, and whatever comes next. Openness is a commercial positioning strategy.

**2. Urban land scarcity emerged faster than expected**
As VinFast sales surged, finding physical space for charging stations in Hanoi and Ho Chi Minh City became acutely difficult. Premium real estate in commercial districts was already committed, and late arrivals paid far more — or couldn't get locations at all.

**The Bangladesh lesson:** Site agreements are infrastructure. A signed 5-year agreement with Bashundhara City or Jamuna Future Park today is worth more than the hardware it will hold. Treat location contracts as capital assets.

**3. Grid stress appeared within 2 years**
Dense charger clusters in Vietnamese cities are now causing local grid stress during peak hours (6–9pm). The industry is retrofitting Battery Energy Storage Systems (BESS) as a fix. This was entirely predictable from Day 1 and far cheaper to build in at the design stage.

**The Bangladesh lesson:** Specify chargers with Dynamic Load Balancing. For flagship city fast-charging hubs, design for a battery buffer from the start — even if it is not installed immediately. XCharge's GridLink architecture is built for exactly this.

## 🇮🇩 Indonesia — The Government Dependency Trap

### Market Status (2026)
Indonesia crossed 5,000 public charging stations (SPKLU) in early 2026, with government targets of 25,000 by 2028. However, the market experienced a significant and unexpected slowdown when the government's EV import duty and tax incentives expired at end-2025 and were not immediately renewed.

### What Indonesia Proved

**1. Policy dependency is a business risk**
Indonesia's market was so tied to government incentives that when they lapsed, investment contracted sharply. Operators who had planned expansion pipelines were left overexposed. The lesson the entire industry learned: government support accelerates growth but cannot be the foundation of a viable business case.

**The Bangladesh lesson:** Bangladesh's current 0% import duty on EV chargers is an excellent tailwind. But it will not last forever. Every financial model for this business must also work at 15–20% import duty. If it cannot, the business model needs adjusting before we scale.

**2. State utility monopoly left commercial segments open**
Indonesia's PT PLN controls approximately 73% of public charging infrastructure — but their focus is large-scale highway and urban public installations. They are not competing in hotels, malls, residential complexes, or corporate campuses. Private CPOs who focused on these segments found both less competition and more cooperative site hosts.

**The Bangladesh lesson:** BPDB (Bangladesh Power Development Board) and DESCO's public infrastructure focus means the commercial/residential segment is ours to build without direct government competition. Focus there first.

**3. Coordination failures caused years of delay**
In Indonesia, disagreements between PLN, local city governments, and private operators over permits, classification of EV charging as a commercial activity vs. utility service, and grid connection rights created delays that prevented many projects from launching at all.

**The Bangladesh lesson:** SREDA, BRTA, DESCO, and city corporations each have a role in EV charging regulation in Bangladesh. Mapping and proactively engaging all relevant authorities for each site — before hardware arrives — eliminates the most common cause of deployment failure.

**4. Overly optimistic targets created investor disillusionment**
Indonesia set targets for 2023 that it missed badly. This damaged investor confidence and made fundraising for CPO businesses harder.

**The Bangladesh lesson:** Present conservative, credible projections to investors and the board. The government's 1,200-station target is a policy aspiration, not an operational plan. Our credibility comes from meeting the targets we set, not from echoing government ambitions.

## 🇨🇳 China — The Profitability Maturity Model

### Market Status (2026)
China has the world's largest EV charging network by every measure — millions of chargers, hundreds of operators, and a government-mandated push for 100% OCPP interoperability. However, the market has matured past its growth phase into what PwC calls a **"margin war"** — where profitability depends entirely on operational efficiency, asset utilisation, and service quality.

### The Deep Lessons

**1. Volume without utilisation is a guaranteed loss**
China's early CPO market was driven by subsidies that paid per charger installed. This created an industry full of chargers in the wrong places. By 2026, operators who chased installation numbers are struggling with assets that generate no return. The profitable operators — TELD, StarCharge, NIO Power — are those who deployed fewer chargers in far better locations.

**Direct implication:** We should deploy 50 chargers at the right 50 locations and run them at 60%+ utilisation before deploying 500 chargers at mediocre sites. Revenue per charger is the metric that matters, not total charger count.

**2. Brand ownership at the hardware level is a compounding advantage**
Chinese operators who white-labelled hardware under their own brand name built recognisable networks faster. When a driver sees the same branded unit at a Gulshan car park, a Banani hotel, and a Dhanmondi office building, that recognition has financial value — it lowers customer acquisition cost and builds the trust that drives return sessions.

**Direct implication:** Use OEM white-label programmes with Autel Energy and Joint Tech from Day 1. Your brand on the hardware is marketing that runs 24 hours a day.

**3. Software is the permanent competitive moat**
Hardware commoditises. The CPOs with durable competitive positions in China are those with proprietary software platforms — because they can offer site hosts, fleet operators, and corporate clients data and services that hardware-only competitors cannot. The hardware is the commodity; the software is the business.

**Direct implication:** The CPMS platform we build or licence is the actual business. Hardware suppliers will always offer lower-cost alternatives. Only a proprietary software layer — with billing history, user data, loyalty programmes, and fleet management tools — is truly defensible.

**4. Reliability at 99% uptime is the market standard**
In 2026, Chinese drivers have become intolerant of downtime. CPOs with uptime below 95% are losing market share to competitors. The cost of downtime is not just a missed session — it is a damaged review, a lost repeat customer, and a potential churn of an EV driver who goes to a competitor and never returns.

**Direct implication:** Remote diagnostics on every charger from Day 1. A 24-hour maximum response SLA for any fault. Pre-positioned spare parts in Dhaka for same-day repair. This is not overhead — it is revenue protection.

**5. Revenue diversification separates survivors from leaders**
The most profitable Chinese CPOs in 2026 earn from: energy session fees, SaaS management fees, loyalty memberships, advertising on charger screens, retail partnerships (people spend at nearby shops while charging), and fleet management contracts. Energy fees alone do not build a defensible business.

**Direct implication:** From Year 2 onwards, explore: corporate fleet management contracts, subscription plans for heavy EV users, and screen advertising at high-traffic DC fast chargers.

**6. White-label CPMS software is the fastest legitimate path to market**
Building a full OCPP charging management platform from scratch takes 12–18 months minimum. Chinese operators who tried this burned their launch window. The ones who licenced a white-label platform (Ampeco, Virta, ChargePoint as white-label), launched within weeks, then built proprietary features on top are now 2–3 years ahead of the scratch-builders.

**Direct implication:** White-label a proven OCPP platform for launch. Build or customise as revenue scales. Do not wait for a fully custom platform before deploying the first charger.

## 🇳🇵 🇵🇰 🇱🇰 South Asia — The Closest Mirrors

### Nepal — The Infrastructure Gap Crisis (2026)

Nepal is the most dramatic case study in South Asia. EVs now account for **73% of all private passenger car imports in 2026** — the highest EV share of any country's new car market outside a handful of advanced economies. This transformation happened because the government eliminated EV import duties entirely and Nepal's hydropower makes electricity exceptionally cheap.

But Nepal has only approximately **1,000 charging stations**, almost entirely concentrated in Kathmandu and Pokhara. The infrastructure deficit is now the single largest barrier to further EV adoption. Drivers outside major cities face real range anxiety. Long-distance travel is genuinely challenging.

**The Bangladesh mirror:** Nepal's trajectory shows exactly what happens when EV imports surge ahead of charging infrastructure. Bangladesh's Runner-BYD partnership and government tax cuts are the same policy levers Nepal used. The charging infrastructure crisis Nepal is experiencing today is Bangladesh's future — unless we move now.

---

### Pakistan — The Early Market Opportunity

Pakistan's EV market is at an early stage — roughly where Bangladesh was 18 months ago. National EV policy frameworks exist (NEVP 2019) but implementation has been slow. Grid reliability, complex permitting, and high upfront vehicle costs remain barriers.

The operators establishing positions in Pakistan now — before mass adoption — are locking in the best locations and building brand recognition that will compound as the market grows. Late entrants will face established networks, premium location lock-out, and customer loyalty they cannot easily dislodge.

**The Bangladesh mirror:** This is exactly our position right now. Every month we delay is a month a competitor can claim a Bashundhara City parking agreement, a Gulshan hotel contract, or a Dhanmondi residential building partnership.

---

### Sri Lanka — The Regulatory Tightening Pattern

Sri Lanka formalised its EV charging regulatory framework in 2026. The Public Utilities Commission (PUCSL) now requires: operator registration, a national charger registry, and compliance with standardised safety requirements. Operators who were running informally are being forced to comply or shut down.

**The Bangladesh mirror:** Bangladesh's SREDA is following an identical path. The EV Charging Guideline framework already exists. Regulatory tightening is not an if — it is a when. Operators who build SREDA compliance into their foundation from Day 1 will not be disrupted by this tightening. Those who delay will face forced retrofits or licence refusals at the worst possible time.
